📖 Synth Guide

Modules

Oscillator
sine = pure/smooth · saw = bright/buzzy · square = hollow/retro · triangle = soft/mellow
Filter
LP cuts highs (warmth) · HP cuts lows (thin/bright) · BP isolates a band. Cutoff = where it cuts, Resonance = emphasis at cutoff.
LFO
Slow modulation for movement. Rate = speed, Depth = intensity. Route to pitch = vibrato, filter = wah, volume = tremolo.
Envelope (ADSR)
Attack = fade-in time · Decay = drop after peak · Sustain = held level · Release = fade after note off.
Delay
Echo effect. Time = gap between repeats · Feedback = how many repeats · Mix = dry/wet balance.
Reverb
Room simulation. Size = room size · Damping = high-freq absorption · Mix = how much reverb.
Gain
Volume control — use to balance levels between modules.
Noise
white = all freqs equal (hiss) · pink = bass-heavy (wind) · brown = deep rumble.

Patching Tips

  • Basic synth: Oscillator → Filter → Gain → Output
  • Add movement: LFO → Filter cutoff
  • Add space: Output → Delay → Reverb
  • Thick sound: Two oscillators slightly detuned

Sound Design Recipes

  • Warm pad: Saw osc → LP filter (low cutoff, med resonance) → slow LFO on filter → long attack/release
  • Bass: Square osc → LP filter → short attack, med sustain
  • Lead: Saw osc → BP filter (high resonance) → short attack, med release

The Architecture of Sound: Understanding Modular Synthesis

Modular synthesis is sound design from first principles. Instead of a fixed instrument with predetermined behavior, you build your sound by connecting discrete modules — oscillators, filters, envelopes, effects — through patch cables. Every connection is a deliberate choice. The routing is the instrument.

How FM Synthesis Works

FM (Frequency Modulation) synthesis uses one oscillator — the modulator — to rapidly vary the pitch of another — the carrier. This doesn't just create vibrato; at audio-rate speeds it generates entirely new harmonic spectra. The harmonicity ratio determines the relationship between carrier and modulator frequencies — integer ratios (1, 2, 3) produce harmonic, musical timbres; non-integer ratios create metallic, bell-like, or inharmonic textures. The modulation index controls intensity — low values add subtle brightness, high values produce complex, evolving tones. Yamaha's DX7 brought FM synthesis to the masses in 1983, defining the sound of an entire decade.

A Brief History

Robert Moog and Don Buchla independently invented the modular synthesizer in the mid-1960s — Moog on the East Coast with keyboard-oriented designs, Buchla on the West Coast favoring sequencers and touch plates. Their philosophical split echoes through synthesis to this day: structured composition versus exploratory patching. Hardware modular systems (Eurorack) experienced a massive revival in the 2010s, but software modular — like this browser-based synth — democratizes the experience. You don't need a $10,000 rack to understand signal flow.

Why Patching Matters

In a modular system, nothing is connected by default. An oscillator makes no sound until you route it somewhere. A filter processes nothing until a signal enters it. This forces you to think about signal flow — the path audio takes from generation to your speakers. That mental model is the foundation of all audio engineering: synthesis, mixing, live sound, and studio production all depend on understanding what connects to what, and why.
